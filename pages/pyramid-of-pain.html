Original Notes

Pyramid of Pain

The Pyramid of Pain is a conceptual model that illustrates how impactful different types of threat intelligence indicators can be in disrupting adversary operations. It ranks indicators based on how difficult they are for attackers to modify or adapt once defenders act on them. The idea is simple: the higher up the pyramid, the more operational burden is placed on the attacker when that element is denied or blocked.

While low-level indicators like file hashes are easy to detect and block, they are also trivial for adversaries to change. In contrast, high-level indicators like TTPs (tactics, techniques, and procedures) are deeply embedded in an attacker's workflow and much harder to alter—meaning targeting them can have a more strategic, long-term defensive impact.

Breakdown of Pyramid Levels

1- Hash Values (Minimal Disruption)

File hashes (e.g., MD5, SHA256) are very specific and easy to generate. Even the slightest modification to a file—such as changing one character—creates a new hash. While they offer high precision, they are also fragile as indicators, since adversaries can easily recompile malware to evade detection mechanisms relying on hash-matching.

2- IP Addresses

Blocking known malicious IP addresses offers some effectiveness, but attackers can quickly switch to different addresses using VPNs, TOR, or proxy services. IPs are often short-lived in attack campaigns and should not be solely relied upon.

3-Domain Names

Domains require more effort to replace than IP addresses. They involve domain registration, DNS setup, and potentially infrastructure changes. However, adversaries can still automate domain switching (e.g., via DGA or fast flux DNS) or quickly pivot to alternative hosting providers.

4- Network and Host Artifacts

At this layer, defenders start to focus on operational fingerprints: specific file paths, registry keys, mutexes, scheduled tasks, or unusual behaviors on hosts and networks. These artifacts are more challenging for an attacker to modify, especially at scale. Campaigns like the Locky ransomware consistently used the same artifacts across multiple variations, making them strong defensive anchors.

5- Tools

Attackers rely on custom or off-the-shelf tools to execute their operations—e.g., C2 frameworks, post-exploitation toolkits, or droppers. Interfering with their toolset can force them to rewrite code, find alternatives, or delay their efforts. This increases friction and may deter them from targeting you further.

6- TTPs (Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures)

At the top of the pyramid are the behavioral patterns of attackers—their strategic and technical playbook. These are the most entrenched and time-consuming to change. By detecting and responding to TTPs (e.g., spear phishing with weaponized PDFs, lateral movement via PowerShell, specific privilege escalation methods), you push adversaries to reevaluate their entire modus operandi. Adapting at this level often requires months of redesign, making it the most disruptive point of defense.

Why It Matters

The Pyramid of Pain is not just about identifying indicators—it's about prioritizing which ones can cause meaningful setbacks for attackers. By focusing defensive efforts on the upper layers of the pyramid, security teams can apply pressure where it hurts most. This results in stronger resilience, better detection strategy, and a more proactive security posture.

Each layer of the pyramid represents a defensive opportunity. The higher you aim, the more you're forcing threat actors to expend time, resources, and creativity to bypass your defenses.
