Original Notes

What Is a Network?

1- What Is a Network?

At its core, a network is simply “things linked together.” Think of your circle of friends all connected by shared interests, hobbies or skills. In everyday life you see networks everywhere:

  • City transport (buses, trams, subways all interlinked)
  • Power grid (generators, substations, homes)
  • Postal service (post offices, sorting centers, delivery routes)

In computing, the same principle applies: individual devices (laptops, phones, cameras, traffic lights even farm sensors) form networks so they can share data. A network may be as small as two devices or as vast as billions. Because modern life relies so heavily on these digital connections—from weather reporting to smart‑grid power to traffic controls—understanding networking is crucial for any cybersecurity professional.

2- From Local Nets to the Internet

The Internet itself is just a massive web of smaller networks. Imagine Alice speaks English, Bob and Jim speak English, and Alice also speaks French, which allows her to “translate” between her English‑only friends and new French‑speaking friends (Zayn, Toby). Alice acts like a gateway between two language communities—just as routers and gateways link different subnets.

  • Private network: any self‑contained group of devices (home LAN, corporate VLAN)
  • Public network (the Internet): the network of all those private nets interconnected

3- Identifying Devices on a Network

Just as people have names and fingerprints, networked devices have two identifiers:

HumanNetwork Device
Name (changeable)IP address (can be reassigned)
Fingerprint (unique)MAC address (burned‑in)

IP Addresses

  • Definition: A 32‑bit (IPv4) or 128‑bit (IPv6) number that locates a device on a network.
  • Format (IPv4): Four decimal “octets” (e.g. 192.168.1.77)
  • Public vs. Private:
    • Private IPs (e.g. 192.168.x.x, 10.x.x.x) identify devices within a local LAN.
    • Public IPs identify your network to the Internet—assigned by your ISP.
  • Limitation: IPv4’s ~4.3 billion addresses ran out; IPv6 expands the pool to ~340 undecillion addresses.

MAC Addresses

  • Definition: A 48‑bit, factory‑set identifier (e.g. a4:c3:f0:85:ac:2d) on every network interface card.
    • First 24 bits = manufacturer OUI
    • Last 24 bits = unique NIC serial
  • Spoofing: Unlike fingerprints, MACs can be faked. An attacker can change their MAC to bypass poorly designed filters (e.g., “allow only the admin’s MAC”), undermining security.

4- Why This Matters in Cybersecurity

  • Device tracking: IPs let you locate and route traffic; MACs tie packets to hardware.
  • Access control: Firewalls or Wi‑Fi captive portals often filter by MAC—spoofing can defeat them.
  • Scalability: Understanding IPv6 is vital as billions more devices come online.
