Types of Phishing Emails

Reconnaissance Emails (Recon Emails)

Recon emails are used to check if a mailbox is active. If confirmed, the address can be targeted later with more focused attacks. There are a few types of recon tactics:

Spam Recon Emails

  • Contain meaningless or random text (e.g., "ajsdhf").
  • Sent to check if the mailbox returns a bounce message like “undeliverable.”
  • No bounce = the address is active.

Social Engineering Recon Emails

  • Designed to provoke a response using urgency or impersonation.
  • Often sent from lookalike domains or spoofed identities (e.g., pretending to be a manager).
  • Common in business email compromise (BEC) attacks.

Tracking Pixel Emails

  • Use hidden images to confirm the email was opened.
  • When loaded, the pixel connects back to the attacker’s server.

Credential Harvesters

These phishing emails are designed to steal login credentials. They usually:

  • Mimic popular services (Outlook, Amazon, DHL).
  • Include a link or button that leads to a fake login page.
  • Capture entered credentials and forward them to the attacker.

In targeted attacks, these fake login pages may match the branding of the victim's organization, making them harder to spot.

Impersonation & Social Engineering Attacks

Phishing works by tricking users into trusting the sender. Social engineering is the core technique behind this.

Examples include:

  • Posing as executives to request wire transfers or sensitive info.
  • Faking messages from HR, IT, or trusted vendors.
  • Using urgency, authority, or fear to rush decisions.

Most phishing emails rely on some level of social engineering — the attacker is trying to manipulate the person, not hack the system.

Smishing (SMS Phishing)

  • Sent via text message with a malicious link.
  • Often impersonate banks, delivery services, or payment platforms.
  • May link to fake login portals or request sensitive info.

Example:

A fake PayPal message with a link like paypal.verification-procedure[.]com.

Quishing (QR Code Phishing)

  • Uses QR codes to direct victims to malicious websites.
  • Often included in emails, posters, or printed materials.
  • The user scans the code, thinking it’s a trusted resource, and is redirected to a phishing site or malware download.
  • Harder to detect visually since URLs are hidden until scanned.

Vishing (Voice Phishing)

  • Conducted over the phone.
  • Attackers impersonate support staff, vendors, or internal employees.
  • May ask for login credentials, payment authorization, or account access.

Whaling

Whaling is highly targeted phishing aimed at senior executives (e.g., CEO, CFO, COO). The stakes are higher, and the emails are usually:

  • Refined using open-source info (e.g., LinkedIn, press releases).
  • Personalized to appear legitimate.
  • Designed to extract credentials, deliver malware, or request sensitive data.

Malicious Attachments

Attackers often use Microsoft Office files (Word, Excel) with macros to deliver malware. Macros are disabled by default, so attackers:

  • Embed fake instructions to trick users into enabling macros.
  • Once enabled, the macro runs and downloads malware (ransomware, keyloggers, trojans, etc.).

Best practices:

  • Disable macros by default.
  • Educate users on recognizing suspicious documents.
  • Use attack surface reduction (ASR) rules in enterprise environments.

Hosted Malware (Malicious Links)

Instead of attaching files, attackers may host malware on external sites and include links in phishing emails.

Two common scenarios:

  • Malicious Domains – Cheap, newly registered domains created just to host malware.
  • Compromised Legitimate Sites – Real websites that have been hacked and used to host malicious files in hidden directories.

Once clicked, these links download malware to the user’s system.

Malspam vs. Spam

  • Spam – Unwanted or unexpected emails, often marketing or promotional. Not always malicious.
  • Malspam – Mass-distributed malicious emails (e.g., malware campaigns, phishing lures).

Example malspam topics:

  • Fake COVID-19 alerts
  • Cryptocurrency scams
  • Diet products or “get rich” schemes
  • Adult content and fake dating offers

A lot of malspam blends in with junk email. That’s why user awareness and filtering are critical.

Extortion / Blackmail Emails

These messages threaten to expose sensitive data or embarrassing behavior unless the recipient pays a ransom (usually in cryptocurrency) or performs some action.

  • Sextortion Scams

Attackers claim they’ve recorded you via webcam or have compromising screenshots. Demand payment (e.g., “send 0.5 BTC or I’ll leak your video to all your contacts”).

  • Data Breach Threats

“We’ve stolen your password/emails. If you don’t pay, we’ll sell your data on the dark web.” Often they include a real password you’ve used (from a prior breach) to prove legitimacy.

  • Ransomware Precursor Emails

Demand upfront payment or you’ll be hit with file-encrypting malware or DDoS attacks.
