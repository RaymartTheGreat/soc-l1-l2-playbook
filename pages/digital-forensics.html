What is Digital ForensicsDigital forensics is the disciplined process of identifying, preserving, analyzing, and presenting digital evidence in a manner admissible in legal and investigative contexts. It bridges law enforcement and cybersecurity, often forming part of a broader investigative response known as DFIR (Digital Forensics and Incident Response).0 child notesOrder of VolatilityOrder of volatility refers to the sequence in which data should be collected from a system based on how likely it is to change or disappear. Investigators must prioritize the most volatile sources first to avoid losing critical evidence.0 child notesDigital‑Forensics Workflow- Label evidence (drive tags, case numbers, date/time, collector). - Photograph device and port configuration. - Log every action (who, what, when, why).0 child notesChain of custodyThe Chain of Custody is just a step-by-step record of who’s handled a piece of evidence, when, why, and how it was stored. Think of it as an audit trail that proves the evidence you collected today is the exact same evidence you present in court or hand off to another analyst.0 child notesLive ForensicsLive forensics is the practice of collecting digital evidence from systems that are currently powered on and active. Unlike traditional forensic imaging conducted after shutdown, live forensics targets volatile artifacts that disappear the moment a system is powered off—such as RAM contents, running processes, network connections, and decrypted data.0 child notesAnti forensicsAnti-forensics refers to a malicious actor's techniques and strategies to undermine forensic investigations. While digital forensics and IR involve collecting, analyzing, and preserving electronic evidence during investigations, anti-forensics techniques erase, hide, or manipulate this evidence, making it difficult for investigators to work with.0 child notesFile CarvingFile carving is a digital forensics technique used to extract files from raw storage data without relying on file system metadata. It allows recovery of deleted, corrupted, or hidden files by scanning for identifiable file signatures.0 child notesEvidence DestructionDigital evidence must be securely destroyed once its retention period has expired to ensure it cannot be recovered or misused. There are several established methods to accomplish this.0 child notesACPO PrinciplesACPO Principles provide essential guidance on how digital evidence should be handled to ensure it remains admissible in court. They emphasize non-alteration, accountability, and reproducibility.0 child notes
