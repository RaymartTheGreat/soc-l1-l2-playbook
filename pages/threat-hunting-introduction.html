Threat Hunting: Introduction

1- Overview

  • Definition:

    • Threat hunting is a proactive approach to identify malicious activity in an organization by actively searching for indicators of compromise rather than waiting for alerts.

  • Purpose:

    • Strengthen security posture and complement reactive Incident Response processes.

2- Threat Hunting vs. Incident Response

  • Incident Response (IR):
    • Reactive: Triggered by an alert or notification.
    • Process: Initial alert → triage → evidence analysis → incident response.
  • Threat Hunting:
    • Proactive: No trigger; driven by threat intelligence and the pursuit of undiscovered threats.
    • Goal: Identify threats before they cause damage and improve existing detection mechanisms.

3- Threat Intelligence

  • Basis for the Hunt:

    • Starting with reliable, actionable data such as:

    • Unique Threat Intelligence: Internally developed intelligence (e.g., IOCs from past intrusions).
    • Threat Intelligence Feeds: Public sources (e.g., MISP) and paid services (e.g., Recorded Future, ReliaQuest).

4- What Do We Hunt For?

  • Known Relevant Malware:
    • Identify and track malware samples that are significant to your organization.
    • Resources: theZoo (live malware repository), Trend Micro’s Threat Encyclopedia.
  • Attack Residues:
    • Traces left by attackers that may blend with normal activity.
    • Requires deep understanding of your environment to distinguish from noise.
  • Known Vulnerabilities:
    • Monitor and hunt for exploitation of product/application vulnerabilities.
    • Focus on zero-day vulnerabilities, CVEs, and misconfigurations.

5- Hunting Methodologies

  • Attack Signatures and IOCs:
    • Characterize threats with specific, actionable identifiers to compare with historical data.
  • Logical Queries:
    • Use queries to filter data for specific indicators, such as vulnerable assets.
  • Patterns of Activity:
    • Analyze behaviors and tactics of threat actors.
    • Tool: MITRE ATT&CK Matrix to map out techniques and their relationships.

6- Leveraging the MITRE ATT&CK Navigator

  • Purpose:

    • Visualize defensive coverage, red/blue team planning, and attack technique frequency.

  • Step-by-Step Process:
    1. Create a New Layer:
      • Start with an Enterprise layer.(add photo: ATT&CK Navigator Landing Page / New Layer interface)
    2. Threat Selection:
      • Use the magnifier tool to search for threats (e.g., “WannaCry”) and select them.

    1. Customize Visuals:
      • Set background colors and scores to visualize threat relevance.

    1. Repeat for Additional Threats:
      • Add layers for Stuxnet (score 2) and Conficker (score 4) using the “+” button.
    2. Combine Layers:
      • Use “Create Layer from other layers” with a score expression (e.g., “a+b+c”) to merge layers.

  • Outcome:

    • A combined view highlighting common tactics and techniques among multiple threat actors.

7- When to Move On from a Hunt

  • Decision Challenges:
    • Unlike CTFs where a flag is always present, in threat hunting you may complete the process without finding a threat.
    • Key: Stick to an intelligence-driven hunting plan and use each hunt to refine future detection mechanisms.

8- Why Threat Hunting Matters

  • Proactive Threat Identification:

    • Discover threats before they cause damage.

  • Uncovering Hidden Attacks:

    • Detect pre-existing malicious activity that evaded initial detection.

  • Minimizing Dwell Time:

    • Reduce the time an attacker can operate within your environment.

  • Enhancing Detection:

    • Use hunt findings to develop and refine additional detection methods.
