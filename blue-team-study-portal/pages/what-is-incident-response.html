Original Notes

What is Incident Response?

Incident response (IR) is the structured process an organization follows to identify, investigate, contain, and recover from a cybersecurity incident. It combines technical actions, decision-making, coordination and documentation to reduce damage, restore services, and learn from what happened.

Good IR reduces downtime, limits damage, preserves evidence, and helps the organization learn and harden systems afterward.

Events vs. Incidents

  • Security event: Any observable occurrence that could affect security (e.g., a spam email, a port scan, an anomalous login). Many events are routine or benign and are handled by automated tools or SOC analysts.
  • Security incident: An event (or chain of events) that has caused or is causing harm data exposure, service disruption, unauthorized access, ransomware encryption, etc. Incidents require escalation to incident responders.
an event is an alert; an incident is confirmed impact

Why IR matters

A robust IR capability reduces downtime, limits financial and reputational damage, and helps organizations meet legal and regulatory obligations. Failing to respond effectively can lead to large fines, lost customers, and expensive recovery efforts. Small list of regulatory-financial examples for context:

  • Google — €50M (2019)
  • H&M — €35.3M (2020)
  • TIM (Telecom Italia) — €27.8M (2020)\

Typical consequences of a successful attack

  • Data exfiltration or breaches
  • Ransomware and encrypted systems
  • Reputational damage and loss of customer trust
  • Regulatory fines and litigation costs
  • Direct operational and recovery costs (external consultants, rebuilds)

The IR lifecycle

1) Preparation

Make it easy to respond before anything happens.

  • Maintain and update an Incident Response Plan (IRP) and runbooks.
  • Keep a contact list for internal stakeholders and third parties (legal, PR, vendors).
  • Define escalation criteria and a war-room/communication channel.
  • Ensure tooling: centralized logging, EDR, forensics toolkits, backups, and network baselines.
  • Train staff with tabletop exercises and phishing awareness programs.

2) Detection & Analysis

Detect suspicious activity, then quickly determine scope and impact.

  • Use SIEM, EDR, IDS/IPS, and endpoint telemetry to surface alerts.
  • Triage alerts: confirm whether an event is a true positive and estimate impact.
  • Collect evidence (logs, memory images, network captures) while preserving chain-of-custody if legal action is possible.
  • Prioritize actions based on business impact, evidence needs, and service availability.
  • Notify stakeholders per the communication plan.

3) Containment, Eradication & Recovery

Stop the attacker, remove the threat, and restore services.

  • Containment: short-term fixes to limit spread (isolate hosts, block IPs, revoke sessions). Balance containment with evidence preservation.
  • Eradication: remove malware, compromised accounts, and backdoors; patch exploited vulnerabilities; verify clean images or rebuild systems from trusted backups.
  • Recovery: restore services to production, monitor for re-infection, and validate that systems function normally.

4) Lessons Learned (Post-incident)

Turn the incident into lasting improvements.

  • Hold a debrief with technical teams and stakeholders; document timelines, actions, and gaps.
  • Answer: what happened, when, how well did people follow procedures, and what should change?
  • Update runbooks, playbooks, and detection rules; implement new controls and training where needed.
  • Share relevant indicators and mitigations with peers or trusted information-sharing groups.

Teams & roles

Large organizations often have a dedicated CSIRT (Computer Security Incident Response Team) or CERT. A strong IR team includes technical analysts and also representatives from: IT, legal, communications/PR, HR (for insider cases), and executive leadership.

Core responsibilities for an IR team:

  • Centralize communication and decision-making during incidents
  • Coordinate containment and recovery actions
  • Maintain and improve playbooks and detection content
  • Track MTTR (mean time to respond) and MDT (mean time to detect)
  • Share intelligence and contribute to organizational security posture

Key metrics

  • SLA / SLO: contractual or internal availability targets (e.g., 99.0 / 99.9). Use these to quantify service impact.
  • MTTD (Mean Time to Detect): average time from when an issue began to when it was first acknowledged.
  • MTTR (Mean Time to Resolve): average time from detection to containment or restoration.
  • Escalation Accuracy: percent of alerts routed to the correct analyst or team level.
  • Alerts per Incident: how many distinct alerts were generated during a single incident (helps find noisy or redundant signals).
  • Incidents over Time: trend of incident counts by period (weekly / monthly) to spot recurring problems.
  • Remediation Time: time from action start to full remediation and return-to-production.
  • Estimated Cost per Incident: rough business cost (staff hours, lost revenue, third-party services) used for budgeting and ROI discussions.
