Original Notes

What is Threat Intelligence?

Threat Intelligence (TI) is the process of collecting, analyzing, and applying knowledge about potential and active cyber threats. This knowledge helps defenders detect, prevent, and respond to attacks more effectively / especially from sophisticated actors like APTs, criminal groups, or state-sponsored campaigns.

It’s not just about knowing what happened it’s about understanding who did it, why, how, and what to do next.

From Raw Data to Intelligence

Most threat intel starts as messy, disconnected information - IPs, URLs, logs, or file hashes. That raw data is only valuable when it’s processed, contextualized, and tied to a threat scenario.

  • Raw Data: One piece of info (e.g., 45.77.33.9 made 1000 SSH attempts)
  • Processed Info: That IP is part of a known brute-force botnet
  • Intelligence: The same botnet recently targeted your industry; you’re likely on a scan list. Add firewall rules and monitor credentials.

This transformation is the core of cyber threat intelligence.

The Threat Intelligence Lifecycle

1- Direction

Define the goals. What should we focus on? Is the priority actor tracking, vulnerability analysis, brand monitoring, or phishing IOCs?

2- Collection

Gather raw data from relevant sources:

  • OSINT (forums, GitHub, social media)
  • Threat feeds (free or paid)
  • Dark web monitoring
  • Internal logs, SIEM, malware sandboxes

3- Processing

Clean, parse, and convert raw inputs into usable formats. Translate foreign-language posts, normalize data, and tag it with context.

4- Analysis

Turn processed info into insights:

  • Who is behind the activity?
  • What are their TTPs?
  • Is this related to known campaigns?

Output should be tailored to the audience — technical detail for the SOC, business risk framing for executives.

5- Dissemination

Share findings:

  • IOC feeds → SIEM/SOAR tools
  • Threat reports → Blue teams, execs
  • Actor profiles → Red teams or CTI platforms

6- Feedback

Get responses from intel consumers:

  • Was this actionable?
  • Was the format right?
  • Do we need to change focus?

This loop keeps the cycle aligned with the organization’s goals.

Why Threat Intelligence Matters

Risk Context

TI adds real-world relevance to vulnerabilities or risks. Instead of “this vuln has a CVSS 9.8,” you get “this vuln was used by FIN7 against three banks this week.”

Prioritization

Two simultaneous alerts? TI helps choose. If one is linked to an active APT and the other is random scanning, responders know where to focus.

Investigation Enrichment

Intel transforms an IP from “just noise” into “known APT infrastructure,” giving weight to what would otherwise be ignored.

Information Sharing

Exchanging IOCs and TTPs with trusted partners helps stop attacks earlier — ideally before they reach your network.

Core Categories of Threat Intelligence

Instead of formal "types," most organizations structure intelligence like this:

  • Strategic – High-level threat trends, geopolitical risks, and business impact. Used by executives to set priorities and budgets.
  • Operational – Insights into active campaigns and attacker motivations. Useful for red teams and TI analysts.
  • Tactical – TTPs and behaviors mapped to frameworks like MITRE ATT&CK. Guides defensive engineering and detection rules.
  • Technical – Artifacts like hashes, domains, and malware signatures. Shared with SOCs and automated defense tools.

These layers work together — technical feeds alert SOCs, while operational and strategic intel shape overall defense planning.

Applications of Threat Intelligence

Threat Actor Profiling

Helps track attackers’ behavior over time / what tools they use, their targets, infrastructure, and motivations. Red and blue teams can use this to simulate or prepare for specific actors.

Proactive Defense

By knowing what attackers are doing before they reach your network based on underground chatter, malware campaign telemetry, or shared IOCs you can patch, block, or monitor more effectively.

Risk-Based Defense

Add real-world adversary context to risk assessments and patch prioritization.

Incident Prioritization

Rank simultaneous alerts or intrusions by likely threat actor, potential impact, or known campaigns.

Investigation Enrichment

Use IOCs and TTPs to expand from one artifact (e.g., IP) to full attack context (e.g., actor, campaign, payloads).

Information Sharing

TI is collaborative. Organizations share:

  • Indicators (IPs, hashes, URLs)
  • Tactics and malware trends
  • Precursor activity

Frameworks & Standards

  • MITRE ATT&CK – Standardized TTP framework
  • STIX/TAXII – Structured sharing and transport standards
  • Cyber Kill Chain – Linear model of attack progression
  • The Diamond Model – Emphasizes relationships: Adversary ↔ Infrastructure ↔ Victim ↔ Capabilities
