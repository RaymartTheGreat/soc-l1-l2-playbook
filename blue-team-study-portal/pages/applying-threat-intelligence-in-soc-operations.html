Original Notes

Applying Threat Intelligence in SOC Operations

Threat Intelligence (TI) plays a critical role in modern Security Operations Centers (SOCs). It empowers defenders to anticipate, detect, and respond to cyber threats more effectively by transforming raw data into actionable insights. In SOC environments, TI helps streamline detection engineering, automate threat hunting, and enrich incident response by providing context, adversary TTPs, and IOCs (Indicators of Compromise).

Intelligence Classifications Relevant to SOC

While TI can be divided into Strategic, Operational, Tactical, and Technical intelligence, SOC teams primarily engage with:

  • Tactical Intelligence – Focuses on adversary Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures (TTPs), often mapped to frameworks like MITRE ATT&CK. Used to enhance detection and prevention capabilities.
  • Technical Intelligence – Includes forensic artifacts such as hashes, IPs, domains, URLs, file paths, and registry keys. These IOCs feed directly into security tools for threat hunting, detection, and correlation.

Operational and Strategic intelligence are typically used by threat intel and executive teams for broader risk assessments.

Intelligence Producers vs. Consumers

Producers

Entities that collect, analyze, and disseminate threat intel:

  • Cybersecurity vendors
  • CERTs
  • Research teams
  • Government agencies
  • Enterprises with internal TI programs

Methods: honeypots, telemetry aggregation, malware analysis → delivered as reports, STIX/TAXII feeds, and platform APIs.

Consumers

Those who ingest, analyze, and act on intel:

  • SOC analysts
  • Incident response teams
  • Threat hunters
  • Detection engineers

Use cases: identifying vulnerabilities, building detections, enhancing alert triage, enriching incident response

Example: A SOC receives updated phishing IOCs from MISP and adds them to email gateway rules and DNS sinkhole lists.

Preventive Controls Using Threat Intelligence

IP Blocking via Firewalls

Purpose: Prevent malicious connections from/to known bad IPs.

  • Ingress blocks stop scanning, brute‑force, or attack infrastructure.
  • Egress blocks disrupt C2 channels or data exfiltration.

Domain Blocking via Email Gateways

Purpose: Prevent delivery of phishing or malicious emails.

  • Block sender domains associated with malware/phishing campaigns.
  • Protects user inboxes and reduces social engineering surface.

DNS Sinkholing

Purpose: Redirect queries to malicious domains toward safe IPs (e.g., 0.0.0.0).

  • Identifies infected hosts attempting outbound C2 connections.
  • Enhances visibility into malware communication behavior.

Combining Prevention with Detection

When prevention controls are paired with visibility, you get immediate alerts on blocked attempts:

Prevention ToolDetection Use Case
Firewall BlockMonitor logs for repeated blocked connections from malicious IPs
DNS SinkholeAlert on resolutions to 0.0.0.0, indicating attempts to reach a known bad domain
Proxy BlockReview blocked web requests for malicious downloads or phishing link access
Email Gateway QuarantineInspect quarantined emails for malicious attachments, headers, or lures

IOC Watchlists and Alerting

SOC teams maintain watchlists of IOCs from threat feeds to monitor environment exposure.

  • Automatic Updates: Feeds (e.g., MISP, commercial feeds) push fresh IOCs into SIEM watchlists.
  • Grouping: By threat actor, malware campaign, or TLP classification.

Example: Malicious IP Watchlist

  1. IOC feed provides known C2 or exploit IPs.
  2. SIEM rule alerts on communication with listed IPs.
  3. Detection leads to containment (e.g., block IP, isolate host).

Threat Exposure Checks (TEC)

A Threat Exposure Check is a SOC exercise where analysts proactively search logs, endpoints, and systems for signs of compromise based on newly received intelligence.

Real‑World Scenario:

  1. US‑CERT Flash Alert on widespread exploitation of “Vulnerability X” arrives, containing suspicious IPs and hashes.
  2. Retroactive Search: Run a 7‑day retrospective across:
    • Firewall logs
    • DNS query logs
    • EDR and endpoint telemetry
  3. If Matches Found:
    • Block the indicators (firewall, DNS sinkhole, EDR signature).
    • Tag affected assets for heightened monitoring.
    • Notify the incident response team for containment and deeper forensics.

Breach Dump Monitoring & Credential Exposure

Why It Matters:

  • Employees often reuse corporate email/passwords on third‑party sites.
  • When those sites are breached, credentials can leak, giving attackers easy lateral access.

SOC Actions:

  • Monitor public breach dumps for organizational domains.
  • Compare leaked usernames/password hashes against internal credential stores.
  • Enforce password resets or MFA on compromised accounts.
  • Use services like:
    • Have I Been Pwned
    • DeHashed
    • Snusbase

IOC & TTP Distribution Workflow

Not all intel is relevant—an efficient workflow ensures only validated IOCs/TTPs reach SOC tooling.

  1. Collection
    • TI analysts gather from: CISA/US‑CERT, vendors, ISACs, dark web, OSINT.
  2. Triage & Validation
    • Tactical analysts filter false positives and irrelevant indicators.
  3. Operationalization
    • Push to: SIEM rules, EDR/AV signatures, email/DNS/firewall blocklists.
  4. Alert → Investigate → Respond
    • SOC triages alerts, conducts investigations, and executes response playbooks.
  5. Feedback Loop
    • Feeding incident outcomes back to TI analysts for tuning and new collection priorities.

Information Sharing Protocols in SOC

To responsibly exchange threat data, SOCs adhere to two core protocols:

Traffic Light Protocol (TLP)

TLP LevelWho Can Access
TLP: CLEARPublic
TLP: GREENTrusted community (ISACs, peers)
TLP: AMBERInternal teams + clients
TLP: AMBER+STRICTInternal only
TLP: REDNamed recipients only

Permissible Action Protocol (PAP)

PAP LevelPermitted Actions
PAP: CLEARNormal ingestion into SIEM, firewall, etc.
PAP: GREENActive blocking (domains, IPs)
PAP: AMBERPassive investigation (no direct blocking)
PAP: REDCovert forensic analysis only
