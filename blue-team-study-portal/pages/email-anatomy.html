Original Notes

Email Anatomy

Electronic mail (email) messages consist of two main parts: the header and the body. Understanding both is essential for effective artifact retrieval and email analysis.

  • Header: Contains metadata such as sender, recipient, timestamps, and routing details.
  • Body: The content of the email, which may include text, links, attachments, or HTML formatting.

Email Header

The header is a collection of lines that provide metadata about the message’s delivery journey. This includes sender and recipient addresses, timestamps, and details about each intermediary server (known as Mail Transfer Agents or MTAs) that handled the message. The header starts with the From line and is updated as the message passes through each mail server.

Standard Header Fields

Every email includes a few essential fields:

  • From: The sender's email address.
  • To: The recipient's email address.
  • Date: The date and time the email was sent.

These fields are necessary for the basic delivery and identification of the email.

Other Header Fields

  • Subject: The topic or title of the email.
  • Reply-To: Specifies a different address to receive responses.
  • Message-ID: A unique identifier generated by the sending server.
  • Received: A detailed list of mail servers the message passed through, including timestamps and IP addresses.

The Received field is especially useful for tracing the path and identifying suspicious or unexpected hops.

Custom X-Headers

Headers prefixed with X- are known as custom or extended headers. These are typically added by mail clients, anti-spam software, or security gateways. For example:

  • X-Spam-Status: YES — indicates that the message was flagged as spam.

While useful, these headers are not standardized and can vary by system.

Note: Headers can be forged or modified by attackers — for instance, the From field can be spoofed to appear as a trusted sender (e.g., contact@amazon.co.uk). Therefore, headers alone do not guarantee authenticity.

Email Body

The body contains the actual message written by the sender. It may include:

  • Plain text
  • HTML formatting (links, images, styled text)
  • Attachments (documents, scripts, executables)

Modern phishing attacks often embed malicious links or scripts within HTML content to deceive users. Attackers may also use embedded images, invisible elements, or misleading anchor text to mask the true intent of a message.

Core Email Components and Infrastructure

ComponentDescription
HeaderMetadata about the email (sender, path, timestamps).
BodyThe message content, which may include HTML, links, or attachments.
SMTPProtocol used to send emails from client to server or between servers.
IMAP / POP3Protocols used by the client to retrieve emails from the server.
MUA (Mail User Agent)Application used by users to send and read emails (e.g., Outlook, Thunderbird).
MTA (Mail Transfer Agent)Routes emails from the sender's domain to the recipient’s domain.
MDA (Mail Delivery Agent)Delivers the email into the recipient’s mailbox after final routing.
