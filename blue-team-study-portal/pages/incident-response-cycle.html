Incident Response Cycle

IR_Playbook.pdf

Incident Response (IR) refers to the processes and technologies an organization employs to detect and react to cyber threats, security breaches, or cyber attacks. Implementing a formal incident response plan empowers cyber security teams to mitigate or prevent damage effectively.

The NIST Incident Response Framework involves 4 steps:

  1. Preparation: Establishing and maintaining an incident response capability.
  2. Detection and Analysis: Identifying and understanding the scope and impact of an incident.
  3. Containment, Eradication, and Recovery: Limiting the incident's impact, eliminating the threat, and restoring normal operations.
  4. Post-Incident Activity: Reviewing and improving the incident response process and documentation.

incident responders, are usually called to action in the middle of the second phase of the NIST Incident Response Framework: right after detection tools or policies have fired an alert, prompting a deep analysis of the anomalies.

SANS Incident Response 101, on the other hand, splits out the containment, eradication, and recovery into different steps, making the whole process comprised of 6 steps:

  1. Preparation: Establishing tools, resources, and procedures to effectively handle incidents.
  2. Identification: Detecting and determining the scope and impact of an incident.
  3. Containment: Implementing measures to limit the incident's spread and impact.
  4. Eradication: Removing the root cause of the incident, including malware or compromised accounts.
  5. Recovery: Restoring systems and operations to normal while ensuring the threat is eliminated.
  6. Lessons Learned: Analyzing the incident response process and improving future readiness.

1- Preparation & Prevention Phase

Core program elements

  • IR Plan & runbooks — short, versioned playbooks per incident type (ransomware, data exfil, stolen device). Include decision gates (isolate vs preserve evidence vs rebuild).
  • Forensics readiness — standardized logging, volatile capture playbooks, pre-staged imaging kits, chain-of-custody templates.
  • Asset inventory & risk assessment — authoritative CMDB with owner, OS, role, data sensitivity. Prioritise protections by business impact (use BIA/BCP to guide decisions).
  • Team & roles — Incident Commander, Security Analysts, Forensic (DFIR) Analysts, Threat Intel, Legal, HR, PR, and executive sponsor. Have a contact registry and escalation matrix.
  • Supply & authority — ensure IR staff have approved hardware, software, access, and the ability to be pulled from routine duties during incidents.

Preventive controls

Identity & access

  • Enforce MFA, least privilege, periodic access reviews, and credential hygiene.
  • Use RBAC, privileged access workstations, and strict admin separation (no local admin by default).

Network controls

  • Network segmentation / micro-segmentation and DMZs for internet-facing services.
  • Perimeter + NGFW (application inspection) and WAFs for web apps.
  • NAC to prevent/limit rogue devices (pre- and post-admission enforcement).

Endpoint & detection

  • EDR with behavioral telemetry, application allowlisting, HIDS/HIPS for host-level detection and prevention.
  • Up-to-date AV (signature + behavior) and local firewall policies managed centrally (GPOs for Windows).

Email & web

  • SPF/DKIM/DMARC + spam gateway and attachment sandboxing.
  • Mark external mail (e.g., “[EXTERNAL]”) and apply URL/attachment scanning.
  • Attachment restrictions based on business need (block high-risk types unless required).

Data protection & backups

  • DLP policies, data classification, encryption (rest/in-transit), least-access controls.
  • Regular, tested backups (immutable or air-gapped where possible) and verified restore procedures.

Monitoring & logging

  • Centralized logging (SIEM) + retention aligned to legal needs; tune detections to reduce false positives.
  • Instrument telemetry for process creation, auth anomalies, data movements, privileged actions, and egress.

Cloud & third-parties

  • Include cloud logging/IAM in IR plans; audit vendor access and add vendor incident scenarios to playbooks.

Physical security

  • Basic deterrents and access controls (CCTV, mantraps, secure doors); treat physical access as high risk.

People, training & culture

  • Regular tabletop & live exercises that include technical teams, legal, PR and execs.
  • Continuous security awareness: mandatory onboarding training, yearly refreshers, and quarterly phishing simulations with metrics and follow-up training for repeat offenders.
  • Encourage security champions and offer small incentives/recognition.
  • Provide anonymous whistleblowing channel for insider reporting.

Automation, intelligence & metrics

  • Use SOAR to automate safe, repeatable containment steps; keep manual approval gates for high-risk actions.
  • Ingest threat intel and map IOCs to detections/blocks.
  • Track metrics: MTTD, MTTR, incidents by type, time to containment, lessons-implemented rate.

2- Identification & Scoping Phase

Identification

  1. Understanding the Role of People, Processes, and Technology
    • Technology detects incidents through alerts.
    • People interpret these alerts and report anomalies to relevant parties using established procedures.
    • Success depends on effective coordination among employees, security teams, and tools.
  2. Security Alerts and Event Notifications
    • Event notifications act as triggers for initiating incident response.
    • Proper analysis and handling require technical expertise, tools, and adherence to defined procedures.
  3. Transitioning to Scoping
    • Scoping determines the incident's extent: affected systems, impacted data, and organisational effects.
    • Effective scoping relies on evidence from identification and clear collaboration.

Iterative Feedback Loop in Identification & Scoping

  1. Event Notification: Incident response begins when an anomaly is reported.
  2. Documentation: All findings are logged, including affected assets and vulnerabilities.
  3. Evidence Collection: Logs, traffic data, and digital artefacts are analysed to understand the incident.
  4. Artefact Identification: Artefacts like IoCs are extracted, guiding further actions.
  5. Pivot Point Discovery: New insights or leads refine the investigation and aid in understanding the full impact of the threat.

3- Threat Intel & Containment

This stage of the incident response process focuses on preventing an incident from causing further damage. It involves gathering intelligence about the adversary and implementing containment measures to mitigate risks.

Threat Intelligence Gathering

Threat intelligence helps in identifying and understanding the adversary. Key sources include:

  • Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS)
  • Security Information & Event Management (SIEM)
  • Threat Intelligence Feeds (OSINT, DigitalSide, AlienVault, etc.)

TTPs: Understanding the Adversary

Threat actors follow certain Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures (TTPs):

  • Tactics: High-level objectives (e.g., data theft, espionage, ransomware).
  • Techniques: Specific tools and methods used (e.g., phishing, lateral movement).
  • Procedures: The step-by-step process of an attack.

Containment Strategies

Containment serves as a bridge between identification, eradication, and recovery. Different strategies depend on risk factors and adversary behavior.

1. Isolation (Aggressive Approach)

Method: Completely disconnect infected devices from the network.

Pros: Immediately stops adversary actions.

Cons: Alerts the attacker, possibly triggering destructive actions.

Use Case: If an adversary is likely to exfiltrate data or escalate access quickly.

2. Controlled Isolation (Stealth Approach)

Method: Allows adversaries to operate in a controlled environment while being monitored.

Pros: Enables intelligence gathering without alerting the attacker.

Cons: Requires constant monitoring and quick intervention capability.

Use Case: If understanding the adversary’s behavior is a priority.

Threat Intelligence Platforms

Threat intelligence should be shared and consumed to predict adversary movements. Platforms include:

  • OpenCTI (collaborative intelligence sharing)
  • ThreatFeeds.io (OSINT threat data)
  • AlienVault OTX (community threat indicators)

By tracking known adversaries, defenders can anticipate future attacks and proactively implement countermeasures.

The End Goal of Containment

The primary objective is to delay or disrupt the adversary’s progress while gathering intelligence. Striking a balance between intelligence collection and containment effectiveness is crucial.

A well-executed containment phase ensures that:

✅ The adversary is hindered in achieving their goals.

✅ Future attacks are mitigated through intelligence-sharing.

✅ Incident response teams have sufficient time to plan eradication and recovery.

4,5- Eradication, Remediation, and Recovery

1. Eradication

Key Considerations:

  • Avoid Premature Eradication: Moving too quickly can alert the attacker, causing them to escalate their actions. Proper scoping is critical.
  • Prevent the "Whack-a-Mole" Cycle: Incomplete eradication can lead to reoccurring issues, making it seem like progress when the problem just shifts.

Methods of Eradication:

  • Automated Eradication: Useful for common threats, but ineffective against advanced, targeted attacks.
  • Complete System Rebuild: Guarantees a clean system but requires downtime and extensive restoration efforts.
  • Targeted System Cleanup: Used when downtime is not an option; requires intelligence-driven, precise execution.

2. Remediation

Key Focus Areas:

  • Network Segmentation: Limit unnecessary communication to reduce the attack surface.
  • Identity & Access Management (IAM):
    • Restrict or disable compromised accounts.
    • Enforce the principle of least privilege.
    • Control access to highly privileged accounts (e.g., domain administrators).
  • Patch Management:
    • Address vulnerabilities that enabled the compromise.
    • Implement a structured patching strategy across the organization.

3. Recovery

Goals:

  • Safely restore systems to normal operations while ensuring security improvements hold.

Key Steps:

  • Continuous Testing & Monitoring:
    • Perform penetration tests and attack simulations to validate security measures.
  • Backups & Restoration:
    • Maintain system backups and documentation for rapid recovery.
    • Use automated setup scripts or cloud-based baseline images for efficiency.
  • Action Plan for Recovery:
    • Near-Term: Address immediate, critical vulnerabilities.
    • Mid-Term: Implement structural security improvements.
    • Long-Term: Develop a continuous improvement strategy with regular testing and updates.

6- Lessons Learned: Post-Incident Analysis

This phase of the Incident Response (IR) process is dedicated to analyzing findings, understanding what worked and what didn’t, and using that knowledge to strengthen future defenses. While it might not be the most action-driven part of IR, skipping it would mean missing valuable insights that could prevent similar incidents from happening again.

Simply experiencing an incident isn't enough—growth comes from reflection and action. This stage ensures that every lesson is carefully examined and used to refine security strategies moving forward.

Technical Overview

The Technical Overview compiles all key details about the incident, summarizing how the attack unfolded, how it was detected, and how it was neutralized. This document serves as a quick reference for security teams and helps improve future response efforts.

Key Elements:

  • Incident Timeline: A structured breakdown of the attack from start to finish.
  • Exploited Vulnerabilities: The weaknesses or misconfigurations that allowed the breach.
  • Indicators of Compromise (IoCs): Any forensic evidence linked to the attack, such as malware signatures, suspicious IPs, or unusual system activity.
  • Containment and Eradication Actions: The steps taken to remove the threat and prevent reinfection.
  • Root Cause Identification: A detailed look at how the attacker gained access and what can be done to stop it from happening again.
  • Security Enhancements: Adjustments to policies, tools, or protocols that need to be implemented for better protection.

This summary ensures that security teams have a clear, actionable roadmap to strengthen defenses against similar attacks in the future.

Executive Summary

The Executive Summary is designed for leadership and business stakeholders, providing a non-technical overview of the incident’s impact and the steps taken to resolve it. It highlights the most critical aspects without delving into deep technical details.

Key Sections:

1. Incident Impact

  • Financial Losses:
    • Was there any monetary theft or fraud?
    • Were operations disrupted, leading to lost revenue?
  • Data Breach:
    • Were sensitive customer or employee details exposed?
    • Was confidential corporate information accessed or stolen?
  • Reputational and Compliance Risks:
    • Did the breach attract public or regulatory attention?
    • Are there potential legal consequences or penalties?
    • How does this impact trust with clients and stakeholders?

2. Root Cause Analysis

  • How did the attack occur? (Phishing, malware, misconfigurations, insider threats, etc.)
  • What security gaps contributed to it? (Lack of monitoring, outdated software, poor access controls, etc.)
  • Were there warning signs that could have been caught earlier?

3. Response Actions & Future Prevention

  • Immediate Fixes: What has already been done to mitigate the damage.
  • Mid-term Adjustments: Security patches, system updates, and policy changes.
  • Long-term Strategies:
    • Strengthening network security and implementing stricter access controls.
    • Investing in advanced monitoring tools and automated threat detection.
    • Enhancing incident response training and team readiness.

Next Steps for Long-Term Security Improvement

1. Continuous Security Testing

  • Regularly conduct penetration testing and red team exercises to identify vulnerabilities before attackers do.
  • Organize tabletop simulations to refine incident response playbooks and ensure teams are prepared.

2. Employee Awareness & Cyber Hygiene

  • Train employees on social engineering tactics, password security, and suspicious email recognition.
  • Encourage a culture of early threat reporting to catch issues before they escalate.

3. Strengthening Security Policies

  • Apply Zero Trust principles to limit unauthorized access.
  • Improve privileged access management to minimize insider threats.
  • Enhance logging and real-time monitoring to detect suspicious activity faster.
