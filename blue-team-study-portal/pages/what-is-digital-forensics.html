Original Notes

What is Digital Forensics

Digital forensics is the disciplined process of identifying, preserving, analyzing, and presenting digital evidence in a manner admissible in legal and investigative contexts. It bridges law enforcement and cybersecurity, often forming part of a broader investigative response known as DFIR (Digital Forensics and Incident Response).

Purpose and Use Cases

  • Legal Investigations: Supports criminal, civil, and corporate litigation.
  • Incident Response: Helps detect, analyze, and recover from cyber incidents.
  • Internal Security: Aids in HR and compliance cases (e.g., data exfiltration or policy violations).

When digital forensics and incident response are combined (e.g., in a SOC environment), the process facilitates evidence handling and response workflows, enabling technical and legal teams to correlate events and build timelines.

The Digital Forensics Process

Digital forensics follows a structured methodology to ensure integrity and repeatability:

1. Identification

  • Identify potential evidence sources: devices, accounts, systems.
  • Locate data: physical or cloud storage, mobile devices, memory.

2. Preservation

  • Isolate and protect evidence.
  • Photograph the scene, capture state snapshots.
  • Avoid altering original data.

3. Collection

  • Acquire data through imaging or secure extraction.
  • Use forensic sound tools (e.g., write-blockers) to avoid modification.

4. Analysis

  • Perform systematic inspection.
  • Examine system/user artifacts, timestamps, logs, communications.
  • Use correlation and filtering to reconstruct timelines.

5. Reporting

  • Document methodologies, findings, and interpretations.
  • Include sufficient detail for peer reproduction and courtroom validation.

Categories of Digital Forensics

  • Computer Forensics: Standard analysis of desktops/laptops.
  • Mobile Forensics: SMS, GPS, contacts, app data.
  • Network Forensics: Packet captures, logs, session metadata.
  • Database Forensics: Detect intrusions or tampering in DBMS.
  • Cloud Forensics: Analyze cloud-hosted services and shared storage.
  • Email Forensics: Headers, timestamps, and message integrity.

Evidence Acquisition Best Practices

Proper Authorization

  • Legal approval required before data collection.
  • Prevents evidence from being ruled inadmissible.

Chain of Custody

  • Maintains accountability for evidence.
  • Includes:
    • Evidence description.
    • Names/times of handlers.
    • Storage and access logs.

Write Blockers

  • Prevent OS or tools from modifying original media.
  • Can be physical (hardware-level) or software-based (OS-level).
